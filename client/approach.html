Our Approach

The Risks of the
Connected Cars

Cars become ‘smarter’ and connected to the Internet through cellular wireless networks, Wi-Fi and Bluetooth. As cars become connected they also become vulnerable to cyber attacks. Modern vehicles have over 100 controllers, or Electronic Control Units (ECUs), that are responsible for running most functions of your car, from steering wheel, to engine, to braking system, and Airbags, to navigation systems. Like any other externally connected computers, ECUs in connected cars have become targets for hackers.
In recent months, we have seen the Cherokee Jeep, Toyota Prius, Tesla Model S, Nissan Leaf and other cars successful targeted by white hat attackers, who were able to get the vehicles they compromised to do almost anything, from turning on the radio and windshield wipers to killing the engine while the car drove on the freeway. In response to the Jeep hack, Chrysler recalled 1.4 million vehicles, taking a hit to their reputation and bottom line.

Karamba’s ECU Endpoint Security:
Dropper Detection and Malware Prevention

Car’s Cyber Attack Entry Points: Externally Connected ECUs

When hackers attack the car, their way into the car must go through the car’s externally connected ECUs. While there are over a hundred of ECUs in a car, three main systems are open to external connections: telematics, on-board diagnostics (OBD), and infotainment controllers. These are the gates into the car’s Controllers Network (CAN bus). Detect and stop attackers at the gates, and you significantly reduce the risk of attacks penetrating into the Car’s network to disrupt its safe operations.

Karamba-generated Factory Setting Policy

Karamba’s technology enables ECU and system providers to define factory settings for each ECU. Factory settings include whitelisting of all permitted program binaries, processes, scripts, network behavior etc. This policy is embedded within the externally connected ECUs to ensure that only explicitly allowed code and behavior (based on factory setting) may run on them.

Early Detection of Droppers

Droppers vs. Malware

When hackers hack into a system they first use a Dropper to implant a malware. Droppers are small codes or operations that their mission is to exploit vulnerabilities and implant malwares. Malware is the code that takes control of the car and may cause significant damage, including safety risks.

Early Detection

Karamba identifies Droppers as abnormal behavior or operation. When such abnormality is detected, Karamba alerts on the attack attempt. It provides early warning for the car OEMs and for the system providers that is under attack. The detection includes source IP and attack path, enabling to block the attack source before it succeeds to implant a malware.

Complete Malware
Prevention

Droppers may succeed to drop a malware onto the externally connected ECU. When the malware – i.e. foreign code – is invoked on the ECU, Karamba detects it as foreign code and blocks it when it attempts to run. There are no exceptions: if it is not part of factory settings than it is not allowed to run.

Benefits:

  • Early warning — alert on attack attempts before they materialize
  • Short Quality Check — Karamba’s software is part of the ECU software. When embedding Karamba into a car, only the system that runs Karamba (e.g. the infotainment) should be checked if it performs according to its specifications. No need to test the entire car.
  • Preventative — stopping attacks upon launch
  • No False Positives — allowing only the code and applications that have explicit permission to run on the controller, creates no ambiguity. Either the code is part of factory setting or not, no false alarms.
  • No Updates — providing security for the current version of the software in the car. The solution is embedded in the development cycles of the controller – when the controller is updated, Karamba is updated to reflect the new factory settings – regardless of changes in the threat landscape, the version of the controller in use in the car is protected.

Retrofit
Now

All recent attacks were performed on models, which are already on the road. As recently published by the FBI existing models of connected cars may be targets for cyber attacks. Karamba enables the car industry to protect existing car models against hackers. By adding Karamba to the software versions of the three types of externally connected ECUs, cars will be hardened almost hermitically against cyber risks and attack consequences. This could be done as part of the software updates delivered during annual car service checks.
